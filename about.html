About RealDPI

What is RealDPI?

Even when two mice are set to the same DPI, they can feel completely different in practice. This happens because every sensor is tuned differently — the actual tracking speed varies depending on the mouse model, its firmware, and how the sensor has been calibrated by the manufacturer.

RealDPI solves this problem. Instead of relying on the labeled DPI value, we use measured DPI data to calculate the exact setting you need on your new mouse to match the feel of your old one — so you can switch mice without losing your aim.

Why We Built This

Switching mice has always been a frustrating experience for gamers. Even with the same DPI setting, your muscle memory gets thrown off because the actual sensitivity feels different. Most players spend days re-adjusting — and many never quite get it right.

We built RealDPI to eliminate that friction. Whether you're upgrading to a new mouse or testing different models, you deserve a tool that gives you an accurate, data-driven answer — not a rough estimate.

How Real DPI Works

DPI stands for Dots Per Inch — the number of counts your mouse sensor registers for every inch of physical movement across your desk. It defines how far the cursor actually travels per inch you move your hand, which is why it directly determines how a mouse feels in practice.

The catch: manufacturers label their mice at rounded values like 800, 1600, or 3200 DPI, but no sensor tracks perfectly. When you physically measure the cursor displacement over a known distance, the true value almost always differs. A "1600 DPI" setting might genuinely track at 1583 or 1618 — a gap small enough to go unnoticed, yet large enough to throw off your muscle memory when switching hardware.

RealDPI is built on empirically measured DPI values — the true counts-per-inch each mouse actually delivers, with manufacturer rounding and firmware variance removed. By calculating the difference in real DPI between your old mouse and your new one, we determine the exact sensitivity setting needed so every inch of physical movement produces the identical cursor displacement — no re-adjustment, no lost muscle memory.
